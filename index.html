proposition. [von Neumann] let $H$ be a Hilbert space and $A\in B(H)$, $\norm{A}\le 1$ be a contraction on $H$. then $\forall x\in H$ the sequence $A^k x$ converges in the Ces√†ro sense to the fixed point of $A$ closest to $x$. that is, $$\dfrac{1}{N+1}\sum_{k=0}^{N} A^k x \to \Proj_M x$$ for all $x\in H$, where $M=\{s\in H\mid As=s\}$.

proof
recall as $\norm{A}\le 1$, that $A$ and $A^*$ have the same fixed points. now, our claim is of linear nature in $x$, and immediate if $x\in M$. hence wlog let $x\in M^\perp=\ker(I-A^*)^\perp=\overline{\img (I-A)}$, and we'll show $\LHS(x)\to 0$. fix $\eps$. pick $h\in \img(I-A)$, $h=y-Ay$ so that $\norm{x-h}\le \eps$. then $\dfrac{1}{N+1}\dsum_{k=0}^N A^k h=\frac{1}{N+1}(y-A^{N+1}y)$ is of norm $\le \eps$ for $N\ge N_0$. since $\dfrac{1}{N+1}\dsum_{k=0}^N A^k$ is a contraction, we have $\norm{\dfrac{1}{N+1}\dsum_{k=0}^N A^k x}\le 2\eps$ for $N\ge N_0$, finishing our proof.

proposition. [Ascoli-Arzela] let $(X,d)$ be a compact metric space, and $(f_n)_{n\ge 1}\in\CC(X)$ be a sequence of continuous functions on $X$. suppose the sequence is uniformly bounded, namely $\exists M_0 : |f_n(x)|\le M_0 \xforall x, n$ and suppose also the sequence is equiuniformly continuous, namely $\forall\eps\xexists \delta : d(x,y)\le\delta\implies |f(x)-f(y)|\le\eps\xforall x,y,n$ (where $\delta$ depends solely on $\eps$). then there exists a subsequence $f_{n_k}$ which converges uniformly on $X$.

proof
let $a_k$ form a dense sequence in $X$. let inductively, let $f_n^0=f_n$ and $f_n^{k}$ be a subsequence of $f_n^{k-1}$ such that $f_n^k(a_k)$ converges. the diagonal subsequence $g_n=f_n^n$ has the property that $g_n$ converges at each point of $A=\{a_k\}$. we will show that $g_n$ is uniformly Cauchy on $X$. fix $\eps$. let $\delta$ be given from equiuniform continuity, then let $X=B_\delta(s_1)\cup\dots\cup B_\delta(s_r)$ for $s_i\in A$ (where $s_i,r$ depend on $\delta$) and finally let $N$ be such that $|g_n(s_i)-g_m(s_i)|\le \eps\xforall n,m\ge N\xforall i$. by our construction, given any $x\in X$ we have $|g_n(x)-g_m(x)|\le 3\eps\xforall n,m\ge N$ so that $g_n$ is uniformly Cauchy as we wanted.

problem. (Gillis olympiad 2015) a row of $n\ge2$ lamps is given. each lamp is initially either on or off. the following evolution takes place : a lamp will be on at time $t+1$ if exactly one of its neighbours was on at time $t$, and will be off otherwise. (each lamp has two neighbours, except the two edge lamps which have one). then it is guaranteed that all lamps will be turned off eventually if and only if $n+1$ is a power of two.

solution
letting $1$ denote on and $0$ off, we find the equivalent formulation : let $T:F_{2}^{n}\to F_{2}^{n}$ be the map $(x_{1},x_{2},x_{3}\dots,x_{n})\mapsto(x_{2},x_{1}+x_{3},x_{2}+x_{4},\dots,x_{n-1})$. (i.e. given by the matrix $T_{ij}=\delta_{|i-j|=1}$). then $T$ is nilpotent iff $n+1$ is a power of two. this is what we'll show. firstly, $Tx=0\implies x=0$ if $n$ is even, and $Tx=0\implies x=0\text{ or }x=(1,0,1,\dots,0,1)$ if $n$ is odd. so $T$ is invertible iff $n$ is even. given the base case $T_{n=1}=0$, it suffices to show that $T_{n}$ is nilpotent iff $T_{2n+1}$ is nilpotent. we give three solutions.

solution $\#1$.
let $f_{n}(x)=\det(x-T_{n})$ be the characteristic polynomial of $T_{n}$ over $F_{2}$. then $f_{0}(x)=1$, $f_{1}(x)=x$, and (as a tridiagonal determinant) $f_{n}(x)=xf_{n-1}(x)+f_{n-2}(x)$ for $n\ge2$. we list the first few $f_{n}(x)$.
$f_{2}(x)=x^{2}+1$, $f_{3}(x)=x^{3}$, $f_{4}(x)=x^{4}+x^{2}+1$, $f_{5}(x)=x^{5}+x$, $f_{6}(x)=x^{6}+x^{4}+1$, $f_{7}(x)=x^{7}$, $f_{8}(x)=x^{8}+x^{6}+x^{4}+1$.
we need to show that $f_{n}(x)=x^{n}\iff f_{2n+1}(x)=x^{2n+1}$. more generally, we have $f_{2n+1}(x)=xf_{n}(x^{2})$ as well as $f_{2n+2}(x)=f_{n+1}(x^{2})+f_{n}(x^{2})$ for all $n\ge0$, as easily proven by induction.

solution $\#2$.
[$T_{n}$ nilpotent $\impliedby T_{2n+1}$ nilpotent] indeed, if $x=(x_{1},\dots,x_{n})\in F_{2}^{n}$ then $(x_{1},\dots,x_{n},0,x_{n},\dots,x_{1})\in F_{2}^{2n+1}$ evolves under $T_{2n+1}$ as per the evolution of $(x_{1},\dots,x_{n})$ under $T_{n}$. ergo $x$ dies under sufficient iteration of $T_{n}$.
[$T_{n}$ nilpotent $\implies T_{2n+1}$ nilpotent] indeed, any vector $x\in F_{2}^{2n+1}$ of the form $(x_{1},\dots,x_{n},0,x_{n},\dots,x_{1})$ evolves to 0 in $n$ iterations. for general $x=(x_{1},\dots,x_{2n+1})\in F_{2}^{2n+1}$ let $Rx=(x_{2n+1},\dots,x_{1})$. then $x+Rx$ is of the above form, so $T^{n}(x+Rx)=0\implies T^{n}x=T^{n}Rx$. of course, $T$ commutes with $R$, and so $T^{n}x=RT^{n}x$, meaning $T^{n}x$ is a symmetric vector (of the form $(y_{1},\dots,y_{n},z,y_{n},\dots,y_{1})$). one more iteration makes $T^{n+1}x$ a symmetric vector with $0$ as its middle entry, yielding $T^{2n+1}x=0$ as we wanted.

solution $\#3$.
[$T_{n}$ nilpotent $\impliedby T_{2n+1}$ nilpotent] indeed, if $x=(x_{1},\dots,x_{n})\in F_{2}^{n}$ then $(0,x_1,0,x_2,0,\dots,x_n,0)\in F_{2}^{2n+1}$ evolves under $T^2$ as per the evolution of $(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ under $T$. ergo $x$ dies under sufficient iteration of $T_n$.
[$T_{n}$ nilpotent $\implies T_{2n+1}$ nilpotent] indeed, any vector $x\in F_{2}^{2n+1}$ of the form $(0,x_1,0,x_2,0\dots,x_n,0)$ dies under sufficient iteration of $T^2$, and any vector of the form $(x_1,0,x_2,0\dots,0,x_{n+1})$ evolves to a vector of the former form under $T$, and thus also dies eventually. by linearity, $T_{2n+1}$ is nilpotent.


theorem (Dickson). if $x_n $ is a sequence in $\N^d$ then there exists $i < j$ such that $x_i\le x_j$ in each of the $d$ coordinates.
equivalently, given a sequence of monomials $m_n$ in the variables $x_1,\dots,x_d$ there exists $i < j$ such that $m_i\mid m_j$.
equivalently, any subset $S\sub\N^d$ may be covered by a finite number of positive orthants, whose apexes all belong to $S$.

we'll now prove Dickson's lemma (and generalize it) using the notion of a preorder.
definition. a preorder $\preceq$ is a reflexive transitive relation on a set $X$.

exercise. let $\preceq$ be a preorder on $X$ and define $x\sim y$ whenever $x\preceq y$ and $y\preceq x$. then $\sim$ is an equivalence relation. moreover, defining $[x]\le [y]$ iff $x\preceq y$ is a well defined order on $X/\sim$.

definition. a well preorder $\preceq$ of a set $X$ is a preorder such that tfec hold :
$\bullet$ any infinite sequence $x_1,x_2,x_3,\dots$ must increase, namely there's a pair $i < j$ with $x_i\preceq x_j$.
$\bullet$ any infinite sequence $x_1,x_2,x_3,\dots$ must contain a increasing subsequence, namely there's $x_{n_1}\preceq x_{n_2}\preceq x_{n_3}\preceq \dots$ for some $n_1 < n_2 < n_3 < \dots$.

observation & definition. let $(X_i,\preceq_i)$ be preorders, $X=\Prod_i X_i$. then $(x_i)_i \preceq (y_i)_i$ iff $x_i\preceq_i y_i$ for all $i$ is a preorder on $X$ called the product preorder.

exercise. if $X_1,\dots,X_k$ are well preorders then so is $X_1\times\dots\times X_k$. in particular, Dickson's theorem follows.
